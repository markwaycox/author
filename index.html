A Memoir is in the works:

CREATING NEW CULTURE

I see it as comparable to acting, playing the same movie role time after time. It’s hard for me admiring actors who are type-casted by Hollywood. I’ve imagined the greatest accomplishment I could make as a published author would be to attempt several genres, of course doing so justly. Up to now, my first novel, One More Rep, has a redemption storyline. The second, Scent of Seven Letters, is a coming-of-age story. And One Stone, my third and most daring creation, is a murder mystery – with a love story tossed in for good measure.

While I was off to a diverse start, I wanted to stretch the boundaries even more with my next writing venture. In different circles around me the genre, memoir, kept popping up. It seemed to me Memoir had settled atop many reader’s favorite genre lists. Thinking about it, it dawned on me that not only would this leap my writing diversity goal forward, but the overall experience would be extremely cathartic as well. Together, the opportunity was simply too attractive to pass up. So, I started reading up on the key characteristics of memoir writing. And immediately, the thing that surprised me most was that all the details in a memoir must be completely true. You can’t fabricate story details for the sake of convenience or to hide your embarrassment. In times of so much falseness in media, that rule actually appealed to me. I saw it as a form of accountability. (And) that is totally lacking out there.

The idea began to saturate me. I was just beginning a yoga teaching course back then, just before the presidential election. My clever idea was to head down to Mexico to an Airbnb apartment with rooftop access. Escape the noise, I salivated. With my beautiful girlfriend’s blessing I would finish the course there and return a changed man. A better man.

Settling into my apartment in Oaxaca de Juarez, things were going so smoothly that it felt awkward. I can’t say at the time that I knew to take this as sign that many things in my life were about to change. But that’s exactly what happened.

I want this Memoir: Almost Never Too Late, to help change the way we are looking at things.
